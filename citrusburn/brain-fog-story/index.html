the forgotten list

a story about fog, focus, and finding my way back
52 · staring at the refrigerator, forgetting why

I turned 52 and my brain decided to retire early.

It started small. I'd walk into a room and forget why. I'd lose my keys three times a week. I'd be in the middle of a sentence and suddenly—nothing. The word was just... gone.

I laughed it off at first. "Senior moment," I'd say. But then it started happening during client meetings. I'd forget names I'd known for years. I'd stare at the grocery list I'd just written and feel like it was in another language.

The worst was the afternoon my daughter asked me a simple question about her school project, and I couldn't find the answer. It wasn't that I didn't know—it was that my brain felt like it was wrapped in cotton. Slow. Foggy. Unreliable.

I started writing everything down. Post-its covered my desk, my mirror, the dashboard of my car. But even then, I'd forget to check them.

sticky notes couldn't save me · brain fog took over

I tried everything. Puzzle apps to "keep my mind sharp." Fish oil. Ginkgo. I even tried one of those expensive online brain training programs. Nothing cut through the fog.

I started dreading social situations because I couldn't keep up with conversations. I felt like I was letting everyone down—my family, my team at work, myself. The worst part? I was only 52. I wasn't supposed to feel like this for another twenty years.

Then a friend said something I'll never forget: "What if it's not aging? What if it's biology?"

The first thing I noticed wasn't mental—it was energy. I wasn't crashing at 3 PM anymore. Then, about three weeks in, I was at the grocery store without a list. And I remembered everything.
